Detroit Music Scene  ·  Spring 2026

Detroit's Concert Season
Just Got Serious.

Ford Field. Little Caesars Arena. Pine Knob. The Fox. If you live anywhere near Southeast Michigan and you care about live music, 2026 is the year to pay attention. Here's what's worth your time — and your money.

By Unshuffled Radio · April 2026 · 5 min read

Some summers you get a handful of decent shows scattered across the calendar. Then there are summers like this one — where you look at the lineup across four major Detroit venues and realize you need a spreadsheet just to keep track of it all.

Detroit has always been a music city. Motown didn't happen by accident. The bones of this town are built on rhythm, soul, and the kind of crowds that show up loud. And in 2026, the major touring world seems to have finally remembered that. From Bruno Mars going two — make that three nights at Ford Field, to Mötley Crüe dragging the Carnival of Sins back out of retirement at Pine Knob, the Motor City calendar is stacked.

On Unshuffled, we talk a lot about songs in context — what they mean, when they came out, why they hit the way they did. Live music is where that context becomes something you feel in your chest. This guide is for the people who want to feel something this summer.

His first full headline stadium tour in nearly a decade, and Detroit gets three stops. That's not a coincidence. That's a love letter to this city.

— on Bruno Mars at Ford Field

Mars' The Romantic — his fourth solo album, out this past February — gave him the runway for the kind of tour he'd never actually done before: a full stadium run. The May 9 date sold so fast that May 10 was added within days. Then August 16 landed. Three nights. At Ford Field. That doesn't happen unless Detroit showed up, and Detroit showed up.

Chris Stapleton in June is the country show of the year. No theatrics, no LED walls — just a voice that could rattle the rafters at Ford Field without a microphone. And the Foo Fighters in August? Dave Grohl rebuilt this band after losing Taylor Hawkins. A stadium show is how you remind everyone what this band is made of.

The Lionel Richie and Earth, Wind & Fire pairing is quietly one of the most exciting things on this whole list. Two catalogs that belong in any conversation about American music — put them together in one night and you've got something that'll go three hours and feel like twenty minutes. That's what live music is supposed to do.

Gorillaz always brings something visually interesting, and J. Cole's The Fall-Off has been one of the more anticipated tours in hip-hop this year. Eric Clapton at LCA is the kind of understated booking that fills up quietly, then sells out completely.

Rock

Mötley Crüe dragging the Carnival of Sins back to Pine Knob on a July night. That lawn is going to be absolutely unhinged in the best possible way.

— on the Pine Knob summer lineup

Santana and the Doobie Brothers on June 18 is the night to pick if you're only doing one Pine Knob show. Two catalogs that absolutely hold up, warm Michigan evening, the lawn open behind you. That's the formula. That's the template.

The Outlaw Music Festival on August 22 is genuinely the best deal on this entire list — Willie Nelson alone is a reason to go, but you're also getting Sheryl Crow and the Avett Brothers in one afternoon. Deep Purple and Kansas the night before is basically the world's greatest classic rock radio block brought to life.

And Jack White at Pine Knob is one of Detroit's own coming home. He doesn't do big, polished arena spectacle. He does intensity. The Knob is exactly the right room for him.

◆ ◆ ◆
Fox Theatre Downtown Detroit  ·  Intimate & Historic

The crown jewel. Restored, stunning, and one of those rooms where the building itself is part of the show. Smaller scale than the arenas — which means closer, louder, and every seat feels like the good seat.

The R&B Lovers Tour at the Fox is the one where you look at the lineup — Keith Sweat, Joe, Dru Hill, Ginuwine — and do the math. That's four distinct eras of late-night slow jams in one show. Late 80s through early 2000s. Detroit is going to lose its mind.

Ray LaMontagne's Trouble at 20 is the quietest, most emotionally devastating item on this entire list. That album holds up. It always held up. Hearing it live at the Fox — that's a night you'll remember.

◆ ◆ ◆

If you're the kind of person who tracks music by era, by genre, by the stories behind the songs — this is your summer. The Motor City has always deserved lineups like this. In 2026, it's finally getting them across the board.

Tickets for most shows are available through Ticketmaster and 313presents.com. Pine Knob's box office opens for the season on April 27. Some Fox dates are still TBD — worth checking back regularly.
